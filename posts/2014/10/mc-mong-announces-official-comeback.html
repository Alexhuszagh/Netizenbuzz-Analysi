  73. Black_PlagueOctober 7, 2014 at 1:02 AM

    "Not everyone wants to be trained to kill."


    Army doctors, medics, engineers, logistics, truck drivers, staff officers, technicians etc. aren't trained to fight and kill the enemy in the front lines. Sure, they learn the basics in how to handle a weapon, but taking on the enemy isn't their job.


    One could say that there are far more non-combat occupations than combat-oriented ones (in this case, infantry, special forces, artillery, armor, reconnaissance, air defense etc.)

    "That's why the Army never gets better."

    Technically speaking, it actually has. Older generations aren't exactly over-exaggerating when they claim that the conscripts of my generation have it much easier than they did.

    The total number of troops that committed suicide in the past decade is several times lower than the number in the 1980s, and probably even more than the 1970s, 60s and 50s. That alone is something to take note of. It's not perfect, but it's a heck lot better than completely ignoring the problem and doing nothing about it.

    As per the Defense Reform 2020, the Army itself is expected to cut down its manpower by 100,000 and cut the number of its current active duty and reserve divisions by half. From that, conscription length is also expected to be lowered, further fueled by the low birth rate and with a focused drive on recruiting more professional troops.

    The biggest elephant in the room is that because the culture is so deeply ingrained for decades, there isn't really any easy way to fix it, and it can only be accomplished over a gradual phase. Either that, or the ROK govt or military decides to resort to extremely draconian measures, which likely isn't going to happen.

    A former member of the 707th Special Missions Battalion (South Korea's most elite force) sums it up just well;
