  49. yahnatiMay 29, 2013 at 10:11 AM

    There are varying degrees of depression. Mild depression is kind of like having the blahs - you're tired, the things that usually interest you aren't so interesting, everything takes more effort; lots of people are affected by mild depression from time to time.



    The far side of it is basically when you stop caring about everything. Nothing is interesting, it becomes genuinely *hard* to get out of bed, you might say things to people you would never normally say because you no longer care about their reactions or feelings.


    There's some evidence that it's genetic - it runs in families. So even if you have 'no reason' to be depressed, that's not going to stop it, nor can you just 'snap out of it.' Sometimes it cycles and you can learn to manage it if it's mild, but if it's serious, it requires getting help and having support from those around you.

  72. myloveisDRCMay 29, 2013 at 4:10 PM

    It's good to know you're doing better. I myself have Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) and a couple of months ago I was in the place you are in now.

    It does get better. But you have to want it to get better. No one is going to hand you happiness, not a doctor, not a therapist.

    Having said that, finding the ideal medication and dosage is very important. I was terrified of being lost in the clouds, unable to face my problems head on, but I am very lucky to have a doctor who listens and started me off low and slow.

    Anti-depressants come in three "strengths" if you will, so no matter how severe your symptoms, your doctor should always start you off with a Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitor (SSRI).

    It's going to take more than surrounding yourself with the ones you love and positive thoughts to beat this. First, let people close to you whom you feel comfortable confiding in know what is going on. You don't have to go into detail, just tell them that you are not okay, but you will be and you just need their support right now.

    Also, and I can't stress this enough, do your research or ask your doctor about a good psychological practice near you. It's amazing how much of a difference it makes talking to someone on the outside. I was skeptical at first, but after 5+ months of weekly therapy my therapist, my friends, my family and even I can tell a weight has been lifted. I'm still fighting my battle, but the difference is I have hope now.



    There is always hope. Stay strong.
