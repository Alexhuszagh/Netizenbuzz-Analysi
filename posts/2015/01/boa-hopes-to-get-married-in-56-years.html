    Koreans look at life before marriage and after as entirely new stages in your life in a way that most Americans cannot even comprehend. you make that permanent commitment to that 1 person for the rest of your life and literal eternity (in Korea you go into the family registry in perpetuality). they see it as becoming a new person in a way.

    the closest Western analogue is "becoming an adult." you are expected to become a new person... you are expected to act differently befitting your new status, you get new rights, new responsibilities etc. your standing in society and in your family changes. as in all things in Korea it's more complicated, refined and in some ways constraining or difficult than the less ornate Western analogue but that's what Koreans are talking about when they talk about "wanting to get married"

    it's not the sex and the cuddling, or going onto that person's insurance. it's about the romance of starting an entirely new way of life and a new beginning with someone else.

    obviously in the past 20 years as Koreans get subsumed by Western culture the reality of marriage is far from permanence. divorce rates are high in Korea now from basically 0 in the 80s. also people adopt Western morals and many of them are already living together... the sex itself is far from anything significant (many of them started having sex in college etc) but the cultural importance and thus the romance of marriage remains and that's what she's talking about.
