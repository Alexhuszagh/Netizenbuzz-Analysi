Beauty standards getting higher in a society already obsessed with looks?

Wednesday, June 03, 2015  nonentertainment  183 comments


Article: People in their twenties, a 'looks obsessed' society

Source: Segye Ilbo via Nate

Article talks about the rising standards of beauty in society's youth. Before, a size '66' was the norm, people are now downsizing to a '55' and even a '44'. Surveys were also done on the trends of plastic surgery and found that happiness levels did not necessarily correlate to beauty anymore because of the rising standards.

1,500 men and women were surveyed on March 2015 by the Korean Gallup for the following response:

Plastic surgery experience
20s: 31%
30s: 16%
40s: 8%
50s: 12%
60s+ 7%

Satisfaction level with looks
49.4% generally satisfied
32.1% generally dissatisfied
11.% satisfied
7.4% dissatisfied

Future plastic surgery plans
47.6% will get work done if a situation calls for it
39% will get work done for sure
9.8% want to get work done but won't
3.6% will never get work done

  167. Everything_KPOPJune 3, 2015 at 11:42 PM

    All I have to say is: it's hard to break mindsets and mentalities that's been deeply ingrained and (almost) intertwined with tradition and culture throughout history, and not just speaking of Korean mindsets but everyone's mindsets in general.


    From the moment we are born, we start forming opinions of the world: on what is wrong and right according to our immediate surroundings, and how we are raised -- primary factors such as our family, friends, school, teachers, neighbors begin influencing how we think as growing children.


    And we carry that "schema" to our adulthood and convince ourselves that "ah, so this is way the world works" until we encounter something completely opposite our (personal) beliefs/notions, something different from what we were taught as we were growing up, and we re-analyze and re-formulate "how the world works" in our minds.


    But since the topic is about Korean beauty ideals, let's briefly look into one of them: lighter skin. In centuries past, a person with pale (white) skin was viewed favorably as that implied that he/she was decently well-off that they did not have to work out in the sun farming all day long which tanned the skin. Thus, tanned skin came to be associated with being farmers and peasants and considered a lower class than those who were wealthy enough to be able to afford to stay indoors all they long (likely with attendants/maids serving them). This carried on into modern Korean society: Korean celebrities would often get into trouble with international fans for teasing about "dark" skinned people. I believe there's a specific word for "dark-skinned" in Korean that does not necessarily directly translate nor has any connection with people who actually have naturally dark complexions (such as Africans, African-Americans, etc.) but it was coined to tease those (other Koreans) with relatively darker (tanned) skin.


    Moving back to the main topic, on one hand I can't really blame the Korean (youth) for the way they think because that's just the way they were brought up and raised in their very homogenized, image-obsessed society. On the other, there are always sources to help them break their beliefs/notions that they grew up with such as internet, foreign books, etc. that could help them learn more about the world. I just think they need that someone/some event catalyst to kickstart them challenging their notions that there are OTHER ways of being considered "successful" or "beautiful" in society other than being what they were taught with while growing up.


    And, as some of the comments below have stated, it's true that Korean society in general is responsible for perpetuating the obsession with beauty-image such as hiring only "model-like" employees for job positions, or even their own parents telling them to fix this, fix that, lose weight / gain weight constantly throughout their childhood and adolescence.
